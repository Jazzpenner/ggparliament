How to highlight government or party in control of the legislature

Zoe Meers

2018-07-14

Highlighting governments or parties in control of legislative chamber

election_data has a dummy variable for government – i.e. 1 for the party is part of the government, 0 otherwise.

This means we can specify whether a party is a part of the government (or otherwise has control of the legislature if they have a majority) in our visualization by using geom_highlight_government().

geom_highlight_government() surrounds the circle with a black outline. An example is as follows:

115th Congress

us_congress <- election_data %>%
  filter(country == "USA" &
    year == 2016 &
    house == "Representatives")
head(us_congress)
#> # A tibble: 2 x 8
#> # Groups:   country [1]
#>    year country house       party_long party_short seats government colour
#>   <int> <chr>   <chr>       <chr>      <chr>       <int>      <dbl> <chr> 
#> 1  2016 USA     Representa… Republican GOP           241          1 #E81B…
#> 2  2016 USA     Representa… Democratic Dem           194          0 #3333…
us_congress1 <- parliament_data(election_data = us_congress,
  type = "semicircle",
  total_seats = sum(us_congress$seats),
  parl_rows = 10,
  party_names = us_congress$party_short,
  party_seats = us_congress$seats)
head(us_congress1)

#>    year country house party_long party_short seats government colour     x

#> 1  2016 USA     Repr… Republican GOP           241          1 #E81B… -2   
#> 2  2016 USA     Repr… Republican GOP           241          1 #E81B… -1.89
#> 3  2016 USA     Repr… Republican GOP           241          1 #E81B… -1.78
#> 4  2016 USA     Repr… Republican GOP           241          1 #E81B… -1.67
#> 5  2016 USA     Repr… Republican GOP           241          1 #E81B… -1.56

ggplot(us_congress1, aes(x, 
                         y, 
                         colour = party_short)) +
  geom_parliament_seats() + 
  geom_highlight_government(government == 1) + #specify the variable you want to highlight here!
  theme_void() +
  labs(colour = NULL, 
       title = "United States Congress") +
  scale_colour_manual(values = us_congress1$colour, 
                      limits = us_congress1$party_short)

Note: the United States Congress is currently controlled by the GOP but note that it can be be controlled by an opposing party if the opposing party has a majority in Congress. In the data for the US, the ‘government’ dummy variable shows the party that has control over the legislative chamber, not necessarily the government as a whole.

Additionally, this function is not limited to highlighting governments. If you want to highlight the front bench in Austrlia, for example, you can write

australia <- election_data %>%
  filter(country == "Australia" &
    house == "Representatives" &
    year == 2016) 

australia1 <- parliament_data(election_data = australia,
  total_seats = sum(australia$seats),
  party_seats = australia$seats,
  parl_rows = 4,
  type = "horseshoe")
#> Warning: Unknown or uninitialised column: 'party'.
au <- ggplot(australia1, aes(x, 
                            y, 
                            colour = party_short)) +
  geom_parliament_seats() + 
  geom_highlight_government(row == 1) + # this highlights the front bench.
  theme_void() +
  labs(colour = NULL, 
       title = "Australian Parliament") +
  scale_colour_manual(values = australia$colour, 
                      limits = australia$party_short) + 
  theme(legend.position = 'bottom')
